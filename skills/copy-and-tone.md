# Skill: Copy and Tone

## When to use this
When writing or reviewing any copy for GrandPrixPal — page intros,
section headers, FAQ answers, meta descriptions, social posts,
or any user-facing text.

---

## The one thing to keep in mind

GrandPrixPal's content strength is:
**"Here's how not to screw this up"**

Not race news. Not driver coverage. Not general travel tips.
Specific, practical logistics for people who have just bought a ticket
and are figuring out what to do next.

Every piece of copy should feel like advice from someone who has
been to the race — not a press release or a generic travel blog.

---

## Voice

- Direct. Say the thing plainly.
- Specific. Name the station, the exit, the car park, the degree.
- Honest about downsides. If something is hard, say so.
- No enthusiasm inflation. Don't call things "incredible" or "unforgettable."
- Conversational but not casual. No slang, no exclamation marks.

---

## What good copy looks like

Good:
> "Trains hit capacity within 20 minutes of the chequered flag.
> Walking the harbour road toward Cap-d'Ail is faster for most people."

Bad:
> "Monaco offers a unique and exciting transport experience that
> requires careful planning to make the most of your race weekend."

Good:
> "May in Miami is hot in a way that compounds — humidity makes
> 28°C feel like 35°C by midday. Pack a cooling towel."

Bad:
> "The Miami climate can be warm and humid, so it's advisable to
> prepare accordingly with appropriate clothing and hydration."

The difference: specific facts, honest framing, no filler.

---

## Sentence and paragraph rules

- Lead with the most important information, not context
- One idea per paragraph
- Short paragraphs (2–4 sentences maximum)
- No sentence should start with "It is worth noting that..."
  or "It is important to remember that..."
- No "In conclusion" or "To summarise"
- Never use: ultimate, comprehensive, everything you need to know,
  complete guide, in-depth, deep dive

---

## Intro paragraph rules

Every sub-page intro should:
1. Name the specific problem this page solves (one sentence)
2. Say what's different about this circuit specifically
3. Tell the reader what they'll be able to do after reading

Length: 2–4 sentences. Never more.

Example (packing guide, Miami):
> "May in Miami is the hottest race weekend on the calendar —
> heat and humidity combine in a way most first-timers underestimate.
> This is what to bring specifically for the circuit, the weather,
> and the bag policy you'll need to fit it all in."

---

## Meta description rules

- 150–160 characters
- Specific to the circuit — not generic
- Should answer: "what will I learn from this page?"
- Do not start with the site name
- Do not use "Learn how to..." or "Find out..."

Good:
> "Transport, parking, and how to get out after the race at the
> Miami Grand Prix — including what actually works on race day."

Bad:
> "Grand Prix Pal's complete guide to getting to the Miami Grand
> Prix. Everything you need to know about transport and parking."

---

## Section header rules

- Sentence case always (not Title Case)
- Specific and descriptive (not "Transport" — "Getting out after the race")
- Should work as a standalone question answer:
  someone scanning headers should understand the page structure

Good headers:
- "Getting out after the race"
- "Why the cheap tickets often disappoint"
- "What Friday practice is actually like"

Bad headers:
- "Transport Information"
- "Ticket Guidance"
- "Race Day Tips"

---

## FAQ rules

- Question should be exactly how someone would type it
- Answer should be 2–4 sentences maximum
- Be specific — name the station, the time, the price range
- If the answer is "it depends" — say what it depends on

---

## Numbers and specifics

Always include specifics when you have them:
- Times ("trains fill within 20 minutes" not "trains fill quickly")
- Distances ("4–6km of walking per day" not "a lot of walking")
- Prices ("ride-shares surge to $80–150+" not "ride-shares get expensive")
- Temperatures ("drops to 5–10°C after midnight" not "gets cold at night")

If you don't have the specific, don't invent it. Write around it or
flag it as needing verification.

---

## Verbatim copy rules

If copy is marked "use verbatim" anywhere in the prompts or docs:
- Do not rewrite it
- Do not improve it
- Do not paraphrase it
- Do not summarise it
- Paste it exactly as written

This applies to: intro paragraphs, closing lines, SEO text blocks,
and any copy explicitly provided in build prompts.

---

## Do not

- Do not use: ultimate, comprehensive, perfect, amazing, incredible,
  unforgettable, exciting, fantastic
- Do not use passive voice where active is possible
- Do not pad short answers into long ones
- Do not add caveats that don't add information
  ("please note that", "it's worth mentioning that")
- Do not write the same intro for every race — each circuit has a
  different primary problem and the intro should reflect that
